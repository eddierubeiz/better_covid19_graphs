The US COVID-19 epidemic by location

About a week ago, I watched Henry Reich's critique of the COVID-19 graphs we tend to see in the media, all of which look to me like a bunch of backwards capital ⅃ 's. Examining work by Aatish Bhatia, Reich notes that plotting time series for exponential phenomena is a waste of time because:

The spread of the disease doesn't care if it's March or April. It only cares about two things: how many cases there ARE today, and how many new cases there WILL BE today.

Henry Reich

Now that many counties in the United States have cases numbering in the thousands, I'm using Bhatia's approach for a county-by-county analysis of the COVID-19 statistics provided by the Johns Hopkins University Center for Systems Science and Engineering (JHU CSSE) using Highcharts. The result is below:

COVID-19 cases by location in the United States

How to read the chart:

Each line plots the progress of the epidemic in a particular place; most places are counties. Places with fewer than two thousand cases as of today are not represented.

Both axes are logarithmic, so the numbers 10 and 100, and 100 and 1000 are equally spaced. (The X axis does not represent time, but the number of cases. The X axis does not represent the number of cases, but the number of new cases on a particular day. To see which day, just hover your mouse over the chart.

What is going on What it looks like in the chart What it means
Fewer than 2000 cases Location doesn't show up yet The epidemic is not yet out of control
Exponential growth A steady progression towards the top right The epidemic is out of control
Linear growth Dots start to cluster together The epidemic is starting to come under control
Falling number of cases The line stops altogether (we can't plot falling numbers on this chart) The epidemic is under control

I hope this chart helps you make better sense of numbers. Please stay home!
